The team conducted comprehensive event and user research to define the scope of their project and establish clear deliverables. Over the semester, they delivered several dynamic presentations to their client that articulated their research, design designs, and prototypes. 
The research strategy included two screener surveys to understand the audience and gather essential data. One survey targeted general festival-goers, while the other was tailored for NASCAR fans. These surveys proved invaluable in gauging people's interests and provided profound insights into the potential reception of our interactive digital experiences.
After analyzing screener results and conducting user interviews with select participants, personas were crafted to ensure our design process remained human-centered. Each persona focused on goals, motivations, fears, frustrations, tasks, needs, and technological proficiency. 
At each milestone, presentations were created and delivered to share research findings, provide possible directions, and bring the client into deciding on the next steps. 
For the Trivia challenge, diverse questions were developed covering key moments in NASCAR history, notable drivers, track facts, and more. Mapping and card sorting methods were used to narrow down and test questions appropriate for the three levels of the game. The final result is a curated set of 15 questions divided into three rounds. The chart of user interactions provided a user flow that guided the UI design process.
The interface design process included low-fidelity sketch wireframes for various pages, including the landing page, question page, multiple-choice page, answer page, end score page, and end-of-round page. 
The next stage was a medium-fidelity prototype that underwent user testing. The feedback we received informed the development of the high-fidelity prototype. User testing provided valuable insights into user response time and screen transitions. Graphics like confetti made it fun and rewarding for users to understand and remember answers.
In crafting the Time Reaction Game, the team honed their design to provide an immersive experience that pits players against legendary drivers. The game tests participants' reflexes and agility and creates a thrilling and competitive experience. The team sought inspiration for the game's art, focusing on incorporating Chicago landmarks and NASCAR branding. The process included sketching and drafting a user flow to map the interactions for the UI frames. 
Programming team members developed a low-fidelity prototype that tracked players' reaction time while incorporating the game art sketches. As the designs evolved toward higher fidelity, user testing, and feedback were gathered to enhance the overall quality and determine which iterations should be included in the final design. Findings included adjusting the background screen color, integrating more Chicago staples into the game art, and simplifying the menu screen design.
The Activity Book and Downloadable Activity Pages feature illustrations, activities, and informative content related to NASCAR Chicago, aiming to foster learning among participants of all ages.
The team pulled inspiration from popular kid-friendly activities and coloring books and then used card-sorting methods to narrow down applicable activities to focus on. Each concept prioritized a Chicago and NASCAR thread. 
The spreads went through multiple iterations and reviews, including user testing with a group of children, which provided crucial insight into the difficulty level of our activity instructions. The students honed their talents and offered custom illustrations at every opportunity. Bellow is an early draft of the spreads.
The final result was a 16-page matte stock publication that expanded on the NASCAR color palette to provide fun, friendly, and approachable layouts. This allowed NASCAR Chicago's younger attendees to express themselves creatively and expand their knowledge of the exciting sport during the event. In addition to the print publication, the web app that houses all team products also has a page of downloadable print sheets for creative enjoyment at home. 

The main goal of the Web app is to easily navigate people to the following links:

  • The three deliverables: Trivia, Reflex Game, and Activity Book pages
  • The Team page
  • The Process page
  • The NASCAR Chicago Street Race
After developing the user flow, team members started designing medium fidelity landing page variations. The goal of the landing page design is to highlight the three deliverables and make them easy to access. The final design was inspired by the NASCAR logo.

The Columbia College Chicago IAM Team's capstone project is a dynamic collaboration involving students from diverse disciplines united in a partnership with NASCAR Chicago. Over the Spring 2024 semester, the student team created three engaging products tailored for families attending the street race event. From interactive trivia challenges testing your NASCAR knowledge to thrilling time reaction games pitting you against legendary drivers, the students focused on crafting experiences that promised excitement and entertainment. Additionally, they produced a captivating and educational print publication to delight younger attendees with its engaging content.

Develop a web-based app featuring family-friendly activities and games. The goal is to broaden the audience, provide educational content.

Research, Ideation, Brand Design, UI Design, Publication Design, Prototyping, User Testing, Programming, and Implementation.

15 weeks, January 2024 - May 2024
